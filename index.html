03

The Pain Points

Four specific things hurting the company right now. Each one is color-coded and answered by a matching solution in the next section.

Pain 01 · Bottleneck
Trevor and Cole are load-bearing operationally.

They aren't just essential for long-term strategy — they're essential for day-to-day function. Every client, every decision, every escalation eventually lands on them.

Pain 02 · Scope Creep
Every project balloons.

Clients ask for "one more thing" and we do it because we can. Every project becomes every service. Boundaries dissolve. Estimates stop meaning anything.

Pain 03 · No Fall Guy
A failure in one area is a company-wide failure.

Every client has so much coverage that responsibility gets diffused. There's no one to blame — which means everything routes back to Cole and Trevor by default.

Pain 04 · Blitz Coverage
Gaps and overcoverage happen at the same time.

Three people on one client and zero on another. We're always either drowning or invisible. Staffing follows whoever's free, not what the client actually needs.

04

The Solutions — Departmentalization

Each solution is color-matched to the pain it answers. This isn't reorg for its own sake — it's a direct response.

Solves Pain 01 · Bottleneck

Ownership pushes down to Department Heads.

Each department has a Head who inherits day-to-day decisions from Cole and Trevor. T&C stop being the default answer to every question. Their calendar reflects it. Their inbox reflects it.

Solves Pain 02 · Scope Creep

Departmentalization — even just the naming convention.

The moment we call them departments, the scope becomes obvious. A client bought "Paid Media" and "Social" — if they ask for something else, it's clearly in another department. The conversation stops being "can you also do this?" and starts being "let's bring in Web Dev." No boundary negotiation. No silent scope creep. Just clarity.

Solves Pain 03 · No Fall Guy

One POC = one owner.

A single POC per client means exactly one person is accountable for that relationship. Wins and losses land on a name. There is a fall guy — and there's a hero. Cole and Trevor aren't the default for either.

Solves Pain 04 · Blitz Coverage

Staffing flows through the Department Head.

The Head assigns people to clients by department, not by whoever's free that morning. Coverage becomes deliberate. Redundancy gets cut. Gaps get filled. The blitz stops.

The bridge. Every solution above collapses into one system: reduce Trevor and Cole's daily workload by distributing responsibility across a structure that can hold it.

08

Stage One — The Three Audits

The real work. What → Who → Where.

Step 1 — Business Audit

Before deciding how to go forward, define what the business actually does. Ideally four or five pillars, called "Departments":

Web Development
Websites, apps, design, logos.
Content
Videography, editing, photoshoots.
Digital Marketing
Managed ad spend, still graphics.
Marketing Consultation
PR, system selling, advisory.
Rank Prompt
AI citation engine, chatbots, automation.

AC and Native's greatest strength is also its greatest weakness: scope creep. Bundling services is wonderful — but even just naming these as separate departments gives the client and the employee more clarity: though we're working on the same project, our roles are completely different.

Each department must have a Head. Trevor and Cole may each head at most one department. The point of departmentalization is to make hiring and systemization easier — hiring a new Cole or a new Trevor is impossible, but adding a new Project Manager in Web Dev, or a new Executive Assistant in Social, is much easier.

The ideal Department Head is not someone who is an expert at the task — but an expert at delegation. They don't need to know how the task is done. They need to know who can do it.

Step 2 — Employee Audit

Now that we have buckets, look at where every employee currently sits. Skill set, client assignments, gaps.

  • Assign each employee to a department. (Some will land in two — avoid it where possible.)
  • Assign each department a Head.
  • Where three people are assigned to the same client doing the same job? Cut two.
  • Where someone is assigned to a client they don't fit? Cut them.
  • Where someone is ready to be a POC? Promote them.

Step 3 — Client Audit

Plug each department and person into the client structure.

  • Assign each client to the department(s) they need. (Web Dev + Social? Both. Just Ad Management? Just that one.)
  • Each client gets exactly one POC.
  • No client has Trevor or Cole designated as POC.

10

The Client Lifecycle: Handoffs Over Time

The system in motion. What each role owns at each phase, and the two moments where ownership formally transfers.

Month 0 · Prospecting
Trevor & Cole
T&C run the pitch, close the deal, and set the tone. Nothing structural has changed yet — this is the world we already live in.
Days 0–30 · Onboarding
Trevor & Cole → Department Head
T&C onboard the team, introduce everything, sit in on the first meeting alongside the Department Head and that department's Executive Assistant. This is the introduction — and the moment primary ownership is pushed down from T&C to the Department Head.
  • Department Head lays out the big picture for the client: roadmap, where we are, how we're getting there.
  • Executive Assistant makes their first move — collecting credentials, staying on top of what the team needs from the client.
  • Department Head also selects the Project Manager who will be the client's day-to-day POC.
  • Expectations are set on the first call: cadence, deliverables, and that day-to-day communication will be hosted by the POC — not T&C.
↓ Handoff #1 — End of Month 1 Trevor & Cole → Department Head
Month 2 · Transition
Department Head + POC
Month 1 is complete. We're now on the retention side.
  • Department Head makes the official announcement to the client: the POC is taking over communication moving forward.
  • Department Head shifts into the "pivot person" — keeping the team on task, handling budget questions, meeting the client every other meeting, showing up for the big picture.
  • POC owns fulfillment. The work.
  • Executive Assistant acts as the department's secretary — deadlines, due dates, where the client thinks we're going, and making sure the PMs are aware of tasks.
↓ Handoff #2 — End of Month 2 Department Head → POC + Executive Assistant
Month 3+ · Steady State
POC + Executive Assistant
In theory, the system can now run forever.
  • POC and Executive Assistant own the day-to-day — client retention lives here.
  • Department Head decides what gets done and divvies out tasks.
  • Executive Assistant makes sure the Department Head and POC understand and communicate the client's needs.
  • Trevor & Cole only come in as the ace in the hole — big fixes, unhappy clients, momentum plays.
A note on structure

We are not losing the horizontal nature of the organization. These handoffs are about ownership of client communication, not a rigid chain of command. Departments still coordinate laterally. PMs still talk to PMs. Executive Assistants still talk to each other. The org stays flat — the relationship to the client is what gets structured.

Early Role Definitions

The structural positions the reorg produces.

Internal CEO
Employee-facing.
  • Employee focus
  • Hiring
  • Boss of Department Heads
External CEO
Client-facing.
  • Client focus
  • New customers
  • Boss of the sales team
Department Head
The leader of the group. When onboarding a new client, the Department Head decides who is on the project. They come in once a month to make sure the client is happy — and if not, they are the one who points the new direction. AC and Nativz are experts on pivoting, and the Department Head sees that through. Fancier title, but the position exists to coordinate the people doing the real work.
Project Manager / POC
The day-to-day people. They completely own fulfillment — the actual work — and are assisted with communication by the Executive Assistant.
Executive Assistant
Focused on client retention. Overviews communication for the department. They can't do the work the client is asking for — but they can find the person within their department who can. One of the strongest relationships in the company will be between the Department Head and the Executive Assistant.
