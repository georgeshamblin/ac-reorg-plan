01The Goal

The success condition, framed two ways.

End state: Cole and Trevor can take a month off simultaneously without the company falling apart — and by the end of Year One, a full year off.
By 6 months
Cole & Trevor take a month off at the same time. No problem.
By 12 months
Cole & Trevor can take a full year off.
Every client
Has one POC — not Trevor or Cole — who answers every message promptly.
Every employee
Knows exactly which clients they own and what deliverables they owe.

Wrapped around all of it: an overarching system that carries a client from proposal to lifelong customer.

02Pain Point & The Bridge

Pain point. Trevor and Cole are essential to the company — not just for long-term sustainability, but for day-to-day function. They are load-bearing operationally.

The bridge. Build a system that (a) reduces Trevor and Cole's daily workload and (b) increases responsibility distribution across the rest of the team.

03Assumptions

The premises this plan is built on.

  1. Time is being wasted company-wide — not just by the executive core, but by the whole team.
  2. Giving Trevor and Cole time to think big-picture will benefit the entire company.
  3. Onboarding before a system is in place is another waste of time.
  4. More direct POCs improve client experience as well as internal experience.
  5. Clearly defined roles and objectives will save everyone time.
  6. Gaps in coverage and overcoverage are symptoms of the same problem — a blitz approach for every client.
  7. Limited engagement with Cole and Trevor improves client experience and looks more professional — same with having fewer meeting members.
  8. Initial setup will consist of micromanagement. That is expected, not a failure mode.
  9. "When it's everyone's responsibility, it's no one's" — more accurately, "it's Cole and Trevor's."

04Lowest Hanging Fruit

Changes that are cheap to make and immediately relieve the load.

  1. Remove Trevor and Cole from most meetings — start with an "every other meeting" cadence.
  2. Limit the number of people on each meeting. At most two people should be on check-in calls.
  3. Implement meeting ownership per client. One person leads — not Trevor or Cole — and is responsible for handing off information and finding their replacement if they must miss.
  4. Early in the onboarding process, Trevor and Cole delegate that ownership to a subordinate.
  5. Clarity on employee job title and scope.
  6. Not everyone needs to be in every group AC is servicing. If someone is not on a need-to-know basis, they should not be in that group.

06Stage One — The Three Audits

The real work. What → Who → Where.

Step 1 — Business Audit

Before deciding how to go forward, define what the business actually does. Ideally four or five pillars, called "Departments":

Web Development
Websites, apps, design, logos.
Content
Videography, editing, photoshoots.
Digital Marketing
Managed ad spend, still graphics.
Marketing Consultation
PR, system selling, advisory.
AI
RankPrompt, chatbots, automation.

AC and Native's greatest strength is also its greatest weakness: scope creep. Bundling services is wonderful — but even just naming these as separate departments gives the client and the employee more clarity: though we're working on the same project, our roles are completely different.

Each department must have a Head. Trevor and Cole may each head at most one department. The point of departmentalization is to make hiring and systemization easier — hiring a new Cole or a new Trevor is impossible, but hiring a new Head of Content is much easier.

The ideal Department Head is not someone who is an expert at the task — but an expert at delegation. They don't need to know how the task is done. They need to know who can do it.

Step 2 — Employee Audit

Now that we have buckets, look at where every employee currently sits. Skill set, client assignments, gaps.

  • Assign each employee to a department. (Some will land in two — avoid it where possible.)
  • Assign each department a Head.
  • Where three people are assigned to the same client doing the same job? Cut two.
  • Where someone is assigned to a client they don't fit? Cut them.
  • Where someone is ready to be a POC? Promote them.

Step 3 — Client Audit

Plug each department and person into the client structure.

  • Assign each client to the department(s) they need. (Web Dev + Social? Both. Just Ad Management? Just that one.)
  • Each client gets exactly one POC.
  • No client has Trevor or Cole designated as POC.

07Early Role Definitions

The structural positions the reorg produces.

Department Head
POC for every member of a department and every client serviced by that department. Determines staffing per project. Ideally the only rep in meetings for the department. Fancier title, but expendable — the position exists to coordinate the people doing the real work.
  • Ideal candidates: Reandra, Nikki, Fatima.
Executive Assistant
Operational support layer to buy back executive time.
Project Manager
Ownership of cross-department client delivery timelines.
Internal CEO
Employee-facing.
  • Employee focus
  • Hiring
  • Boss of Department Heads
External CEO
Client-facing.
  • Client focus
  • New customers
  • Boss of the sales team

08Key Terms

Department
An area of products or services that could stand alone as a business. May be assisted by other departments, but is ideally able to autonomously deliver results to a client.
Department Head
The POC for every member of a department and every client serviced by that department. Determines staffing to a given project and does hands-on work to deliver. Ideally the only rep in meetings for the department. Though the title is fancier, the position is expendable — it exists to coordinate the people doing the real work. Ideal candidates: Reandra, Nikki, Fatima.
